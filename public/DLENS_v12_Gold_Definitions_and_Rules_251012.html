3) What is “Disruption”?

Plain-English Summary: A paradigm shift changes how a thing is built, delivered, improved, and paid for. It is not just “better”—it makes the old way stop making sense.

Detailed & Examples: Positive (paradigm shift): horse & cart → motor car; DVDs → streaming; human driving → full self‑driving. Not paradigm shifts (just upgrades): 1080p → 4K TV; slightly better ICE car. For each case, identify architectural change, cost curve reset, software/data role, and business model shift.

4) Convergence Thesis & Secondary Opportunities

Plain-English Summary: Today, many big changes are happening at once. AI speeds up progress in EVs, eVTOL aircraft, gene editing, LLM data centers, robotics, and energy storage. Some winners build the new systems; others supply the picks‑and‑shovels.

Detailed: List of vectors: AI models & tooling; EVs & autonomy; eVTOL; robotics; gene editing; semis & accelerators; LLM data‑center build‑outs; energy storage & smart grids. Define ecosystem beneficiaries (enablers) and why they can be attractive risk‑adjusted investments.

5) DUU ⇄ Price Linkage (v12 Principle)

Plain-English Summary: DUU and the price prediction say the same thing two ways. DUU is the idea strength; the price is that idea expressed as dollars.

Detailed with math: The Most‑Likely Price equals Multiple × CSP. Upside% = (Multiple − 1) × 100%. Example: CSP = $100; Multiple = 2.5× ⇒ Most‑Likely Price = $250 (Upside 150%). The DUU score (0–10) must be consistent with the central price view for the same horizon.

6) DDI Method (Depth & Breadth + CPS)

Plain-English Summary: DDI shows how wide and how deep the change is. We look at how many parts of the system flip (breadth) and how strong each flip is (depth). A CPS bonus rewards truly clean‑sheet systems.

Detailed: Breadth% = share of disruption vectors scoring ≥7/10. Depth = average score (0–10) across those vectors. CPS bonus = 0, 5, or 10 depending on clean‑sheet platform strength.
Formula: DDI = 0.4×Breadth% + 0.5×Depth×10 + CPS.
Tiers: 0–24 Incremental • 25–49 Partial • 50–74 Major • 75–100 System Replatformer. Include 2–3 numeric worked examples.

7) Feasibility Mix (XDLens‑E/S, Science Dependency, Mix E/S)

Plain-English Summary: If success needs mostly engineering work, risk is lower. If it needs science breakthroughs, risk is higher. We show the mix clearly.

Detailed: Score XDLens‑E and XDLens‑S on 0–10. Science Dependency S (0–10) converts to MixE = 100 − 5×S, MixS = 5×S. Low S ⇒ incremental engineering path; high S ⇒ breakthrough path. Present Mix E/S% and the implication for timelines and capital.

8) Frogfree, FEP, FVU

Plain-English Summary: Frogfree means fewer blind spots. FEP shows leader skill. FVU is value the market hasn’t priced yet.

Detailed: Frogfree (0–10) = freedom from bounded‑thinking (Hunt shows only this number). FEP (1–7) reflects founder/leadership execution advantage. FVU (1–10) captures under‑recognized future value. Give short scoring rubrics for each.

9) Two‑Source CSP Rule (Operational)

Plain-English Summary: Always use two public sources for the stock price and stamp the time. No placeholders.

Detailed: Acceptable sources include major finance portals and exchange feeds. Record both sources, the price, and the America/Detroit timestamp inline in the report. In Spotlight, show it in the At‑a‑Glance chips; in Hunt, include it in the Notes column.

10) SSOT Process (Hunt ⇄ Spotlight)

Plain-English Summary: Hunt finds the best names. Spotlight explains one name deeply. Numbers must always match between them.

Detailed: Hunt is the front door: inclusion and ranking by DUU (tie‑break DDI). When a Spotlight is generated for a ticker, it must reuse exactly the same metrics as its Hunt row (SSOT). No placeholders; publish‑ready only.
